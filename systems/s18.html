18 · Web Crawler

Hard constraint: the URL frontier must be distributed (too big for one node) yet ordered (prioritized) — a distributed priority queue is non-trivial.
Primitives exercised: P2, P1, P4 (DNS caching), graph traversal.

URL frontier (priority queue by freshness); politeness (robots.txt, per-domain rate limit); near-duplicate detection (SimHash/MinHash); mandatory DNS caching; recrawl scheduling via change-frequency backoff.

1 · Requirements & scale

Functional: traverse the web graph, fetch pages, extract links, store content for indexing, and recrawl as pages change. Non-functional: web-scale throughput, politeness toward sites, deduplication, and prioritization of what to crawl next.

Back-of-envelope target 1,000,000,000 pages, recrawl periodically 1,000 pages/s sustained ~ 86M/day -> ~12 days for a billion each page: DNS + fetch + parse + dedup + enqueue new links politeness: <= 1 concurrent / small rate per domain

2 · High-level architecture

graph LR
  F[(URL frontier: prioritized + per-domain)] --> FET[fetchers]
  FET --> DNS[DNS cache]
  FET --> PARSE[parse + extract links]
  PARSE --> DEDUP{seen? near-dup?}
  DEDUP -->|new| F
  PARSE --> STORE[(content store)]

3 · The hard constraint, in depth

The URL frontier must be distributed (it’s far too large for one node) yet ordered (crawl high-priority/fresh pages first) and polite (never hammer one domain). A distributed priority queue with per-domain rate limiting is non-trivial — the practical answers are per-domain queues (a sorted set per host) or Kafka topics with priority, plus a scheduler that releases URLs respecting each domain’s politeness delay.

4 · Component deep dives

Frontier & politeness

Two-level: a front queue orders by priority/freshness; a back queue per domain enforces politeness (one in flight, respect robots.txt and crawl-delay). Partition by domain so a host’s pages stay together (P2).

Deduplication

Exact URL dedup (seen-set, often a Bloom filter, P17) plus near-duplicate content detection via SimHash/MinHash so mirror pages and boilerplate aren’t re-stored.

DNS caching

Millions of lookups make DNS a bottleneck; an aggressive local DNS cache is mandatory (P4).

Recrawl scheduling

Pages change at different rates; schedule recrawls with exponential backoff keyed to observed change frequency — crawl news hourly, an archive monthly.

5 · Real-world example

Googlebot / Mercator

The architecture traces to the Mercator crawler (Heydon & Najork) and the original Google crawler (Brin & Page): a prioritized URL frontier, per-host politeness queues, a seen-URL set, content fingerprinting to avoid duplicates, and a DNS cache. Modern Googlebot adds crawl-budget management (how much to crawl a site without overloading it) and change-frequency-driven recrawl scheduling. The defining challenge is the same: a frontier that is simultaneously huge, ordered, and polite.

6 · Tradeoffs & alternatives

Breadth-first is simple but ignores importance; priority crawling fetches valuable pages first at the cost of frontier complexity. Aggressive recrawl keeps the index fresh but wastes bandwidth on stable pages. Bloom-filter dedup is memory-cheap but admits rare false positives (a page skipped).

7 · Key talking points

  • The frontier: distributed + prioritized + polite is the wall.
  • Per-domain politeness queues; respect robots.txt (P2).
  • Exact + near-duplicate dedup (Bloom + SimHash) (P17).
  • Mandatory DNS caching (P4).
  • Change-frequency-based recrawl scheduling.
