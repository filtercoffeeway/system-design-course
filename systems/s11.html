11 · Notification System

Hard constraint: at-least-once is easy; exactly-once is near-impossible — you can't see whether FCM/Twilio actually delivered. Design for idempotency.
Primitives exercised: P9, P5, P1/priority, P16 (channel limits).

Priority queues (critical vs marketing); idempotency keys; channel-specific rate limits; scheduled/delayed delivery with a scanner; per-channel preference checks before send.

1 · Requirements & scale

Functional: deliver messages across channels (push, SMS, email, in-app) with priorities, scheduling, user preferences, and at-least-once delivery. Non-functional: high throughput, no double-sends, respect external provider limits, isolate slow/marketing traffic from urgent alerts.

Back-of-envelope 500,000,000 notifications/day ~ 6,000/s avg, large campaign spikes channels: FCM, APNs, Twilio (SMS), SES (email) -- each with its own rate limits preference + dedup state per user-channel

2 · High-level architecture

  PQ --> W[workers]
  W --> PREF{prefs + dedup}
  PREF --> CH1[push: FCM/APNs]

3 · The hard constraint, in depth

At-least-once is easy; exactly-once is effectively impossible because the external channels (FCM, APNs, Twilio) are black boxes — you can’t know whether a push truly reached the device. So you design for idempotency (P9): a dedup key per (user, event) so retries don’t double-send, plus tracking to reconcile. You also must honor each channel’s rate limits or get throttled/banned.

4 · Component deep dives

Priority & isolation (P1/P16)

Separate queues so a 10M-recipient marketing blast can’t starve a 2FA code or a "your driver arrived" alert. Workers drain high-priority first; per-channel buffers apply backpressure (P16).

Idempotency & retries (P9)

Dedup key written before send; retries on transient channel failures with backoff; dead-letter after N attempts — see P9.

Channel rate limits

FCM/APNs/Twilio each have per-second/per-account ceilings; a per-channel rate limiter (S1) and token-bucket pacing keep you under them.

Scheduling & preferences

Future-dated notifications go to a delayed store scanned by a timer (the same delayed -queue pattern as the task scheduler). Per-channel opt-outs and quiet-hours are checked at send time, per channel, not once globally.

5 · Real-world example

Uber

Uber has described its push platform handling on the order of hundreds of millions of notifications a day across push/SMS/email, with the same shape as above: a unified API in front of channel-specific adapters (APNs, FCM, SMS providers), priority separation so operational messages (trip updates) beat promotional ones, idempotency to avoid duplicate trip alerts, and per-provider rate control. The defining reality is that the channels are third parties you must integrate with and pace, so the platform is mostly about reliability, dedup, and provider management rather than novel storage.

6 · Tradeoffs & alternatives

Push for speed vs SMS/email for reach and reliability. Aggressive retries improve delivery but risk duplicates and cost; idempotency lets you retry safely. Real-time vs batched/digest delivery trades immediacy for volume control.

7 · Key talking points

  • Design for at-least-once + idempotent dedup, not exactly-once (P9).
  • Priority queues isolate urgent from bulk (P1, P16).
  • Respect per-channel provider rate limits (S1).
  • Delayed queue + scanner for scheduling (S12).
  • Check preferences per channel at send time.
