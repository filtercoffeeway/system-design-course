3 · Key-Value Store (Redis / DynamoDB-style)

Hard constraint: CAP felt viscerally — under partition, return stale (AP) or error (CP). The choice is the application's, and you must reason about it explicitly.
Primitives exercised: P17 (LSM/WAL/Bloom), P2 (consistent hashing), P3, P4.

LSM vs B-tree; WAL for crash recovery; consistent hashing; single-/multi-leader/leaderless quorum; eventual consistency + vector clocks; Bloom filters to skip disk on misses.

1 · Requirements & scale

Functional: get(key) / put(key, value) at planet scale, always-on. Non-functional: very high throughput, low latency, incremental scalability, and tolerance of node and network failure — with a deliberate consistency choice.

Back-of-envelope 100,000,000,000 keys x 1 KB = 100 TB logical replication factor 3 = 300 TB 1,000,000 ops/s / 50,000 ops/s per node ~ 20+ nodes just for throughput

2 · High-level architecture

graph LR
  C[client] --> CO[coordinator node]
  CO -->|consistent hash ring| N1[replica 1]
  CO --> N2[replica 2]
  CO --> N3[replica 3]

Keys are placed on a consistent-hash ring (P2) and replicated to the next N nodes. Any node can coordinate a request; writes/reads use quorums.

3 · The hard constraint, in depth

CAP is felt viscerally here. Under a network partition you must either return a possibly-stale value (AP) or refuse to answer (CP). Dynamo-style stores choose availability and expose tunable quorums (R + W > N for read-your-writes overlap); a CP store like a Raft-replicated KV chooses consistency and rejects writes in a minority partition. The interviewer wants you to pick deliberately and justify it from the use case.

4 · Component deep dives

Storage engine (P17)

LSM-tree (memtable → SSTables → compaction) for write-heavy workloads, WAL for durability, Bloom filters to skip disk on misses — see P17.

Partitioning & replication (P2, P3)

Consistent hashing with virtual nodes spreads data and makes scaling incremental; each key’s value is replicated to N successors. Quorum reads/writes and tunable consistency are the P3 dial.

Conflict handling

With leaderless writes, two clients can update concurrently. Vector clocks / version vectors detect causally-concurrent versions; the app (or last-writer-wins) reconciles. Hinted handoff lets a temporarily-down replica’s writes be parked on a neighbor and replayed later; Merkle-tree anti-entropy repairs divergent replicas in the background.

5 · Real-world example

Amazon Dynamo / DynamoDB

Amazon’s 2007 Dynamo paper is the blueprint: consistent hashing + virtual nodes, quorum reads/writes (R + W > N), vector clocks for conflict detection, hinted handoff for transient failures, and Merkle trees for anti-entropy — all chosen to keep the shopping cart writable even during failures (an "add to cart" must never be rejected). The managed descendant, DynamoDB, keeps the partitioned + replicated core (three AZs) and exposes the consistency dial as eventually- vs strongly-consistent reads. Cassandra and Riak are open-source embodiments of the same paper.

6 · Tradeoffs & alternatives

Leaderless AP maximizes availability but pushes conflict handling onto you. A Raft/Paxos-replicated CP KV (etcd-like) gives linearizable reads but sacrifices availability in a partition and write throughput. The hot-key problem (P2) — a single wide partition — is the classic operational failure.

7 · Key talking points

  • Open with the CAP choice and justify AP vs CP from the use case.
  • Consistent hashing + vnodes for elastic partitioning (P2).
  • Quorums (R + W > N) as the consistency dial (P3).
  • LSM + WAL + Bloom internals (P17).
  • Vector clocks, hinted handoff, Merkle anti-entropy for AP correctness.
