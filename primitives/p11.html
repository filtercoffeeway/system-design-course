P11 · CDN & edge caching

What it is: serving bytes from a node close to the user, so origin only sees cache misses.
Reach for it when: large static assets at high concurrency, or any read-heavy content with geographic spread.
The lever: origin populates the CDN; CDN serves users; the design question becomes "what populates the edge and how is it invalidated?" 301 (cached forever, cheap, kills analytics) vs 302 (always hits you, countable).
New problem it hands you: edge staleness/invalidation; cache-miss stampede on origin; cost of cache fill for long-tail content.
Exemplified by: Video streaming, URL shortener.

1 · The problem it solves

Serving large or popular content from a single origin to a global audience is slow (speed of light) and ruinously expensive (origin bandwidth). A CDN caches content at edge locations near users so the origin only handles cache misses.

2 · How it works

graph LR
  U[user] --> E[edge PoP near user]
  E -->|hit| U
  E -->|miss: pull once| O[origin]
  O --> E

The user hits the nearest point of presence; on a hit it’s served from the edge, on a miss the edge pulls from origin once and caches it. The design question becomes "what populates the edge, and how is it invalidated?" — not "how do I serve the bytes?" For redirects, 301 is cached by browsers (cheap, but you lose click analytics) while 302 always returns to you (countable, pricier).

3 · The new bottleneck it hands you

Invalidation/staleness at the edge (purging across hundreds of PoPs is its own system), cache-miss stampede on origin when a popular object expires everywhere at once, and the cost of filling the long tail (rarely-watched content that never gets a hit to amortize the fill).

4 · Real-world example

Netflix Open Connect

Netflix runs its own CDN, Open Connect, and places purpose-built caching appliances inside ISP networks and internet exchanges — as close to subscribers as physically possible. Crucially it pre-positions content: during off-peak hours Open Connect proactively pushes the titles predicted to be popular in each region onto those appliances, so at peak almost every byte is served from an edge box on the user’s own ISP and the origin serves almost nothing. That is "what populates the edge?" answered by prediction rather than on-demand misses. See Video streaming.

5 · Applying it in a design

For any static/large/read-heavy asset: "CDN at the edge; origin only takes misses; the real work is the fill and invalidation strategy." Pair with caching patterns at the edge.

6 · Pitfalls

  • Designing the serving path but ignoring the fill/invalidation path.
  • Synchronized TTL expiry causing origin stampede.
