17 · Payment System

Hard constraint: you can't span a distributed transaction across your DB and an external processor — only your side is controllable. Saga + idempotency + reconciliation is the standard answer.
Primitives exercised: P14, P9, P3 (strong consistency).

Double-entry bookkeeping (entries sum to zero → bug detection); idempotency keys on payment APIs; saga with compensations; reconciliation as the correctness net; exactly-once via a transactional idempotency table.

1 · Requirements & scale

Functional: move money correctly — charge, transfer, refund — integrating external processors/banks. Non-functional: correctness above all (no lost or double money), auditability, and the one domain where eventual consistency is unacceptable.

Back-of-envelope correctness target: zero money created/destroyed (must reconcile to the cent) external dependencies: card networks, banks, Stripe -- you control only your side throughput secondary to correctness + auditability

2 · High-level architecture

3 · The hard constraint, in depth

You cannot wrap a distributed transaction across your database and an external processor (Stripe, a bank) — you only control your side and can’t two-phase-commit with VISA. So the standard answer is saga + idempotency + reconciliation: drive the steps with compensations (P14), make every call idempotent (P9), and run a batch that compares your ledger to the processor’s statement to catch and fix any drift.

4 · Component deep dives

Double-entry ledger

Every transaction is two entries (a debit and a credit) that must sum to zero. This isn’t accounting nostalgia — it’s a built-in invariant: if the books don’t balance, you have a bug. The ledger is append-only and immutable (corrections are new entries, never edits).

Idempotency (P9)

Idempotency keys on payment APIs so a retried charge doesn’t double-charge; the result is stored transactionally with the ledger write — see P9.

Saga / compensation (P14)

A payment is multi-step (authorize → capture → notify); each step has a compensating action (void/refund) if a later step fails (P14).

Reconciliation

A nightly job matches your ledger against the processor/bank statement line by line; mismatches are flagged and resolved. This is the real correctness guarantee — not a real-time one.

5 · Real-world example

Stripe / Uber ledger

Stripe’s API is built on idempotency keys (P9) precisely so charge retries are safe, and internally money movement is modeled as immutable double-entry ledger entries with reconciliation against banks/card networks. Uber built LedgerStore, a purpose-built immutable double-entry ledger handling tens of billions of entries to record every financial event (fares, payouts, refunds) with strong auditability. Both show the pattern: idempotent APIs, an append-only double-entry ledger as the source of truth, sagas for multi-step flows, and reconciliation as the final net.

6 · Tradeoffs & alternatives

Strong consistency and auditability are non-negotiable, so you accept lower throughput and more process than other domains. 2PC across your services is possible but blocks; a saga is preferred. Real-time guarantees are impossible against external parties — reconciliation is how you make it right after the fact.

7 · Key talking points

  • Double-entry ledger: entries sum to zero = built-in bug detector.
  • Idempotency keys to prevent double-charge (P9).
  • Saga + compensation for multi-step flows (P14).
  • Reconciliation is the real correctness mechanism, not real-time.
  • You only control your side; no distributed txn with the bank.
